Overview

The Security Council is one of the six main organs of the United Nations (UN). It is responsible for maintaining international peace and security, and has the authority to take collective measures to prevent and remove threats to peace. The Security Council consists of 15 members, including 5 permanent members with veto power (China, France, Russia, the United Kingdom, and the United States) and 10 elected members that serve for two-year terms.

The Security Council has several functions, including: Imposing sanctions on countries or individuals that threaten international peace and security; establishing UN peacekeeping missions to maintain peace in conflict-affected areas; authorizing the use of force, such as military intervention or sanctions; recommending measures to maintain or restore international peace and security; investigating any situation that might threaten international peace and security.

The Security Council meets regularly to discuss and address current issues, and can also convene an emergency meeting if necessary. Decisions of the Security Council are made by a majority vote of its members, but a veto by any of the permanent members can block a resolution from being adopted. The Security Council also has the ability to refer situations to the International Criminal Court (ICC) for investigation and prosecution.

Overall, the Security Council plays a crucial role in maintaining international peace and security, and its decisions have significant impacts on global affairs

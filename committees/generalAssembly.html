Overview

The United Nations General Assembly is a vital forum for member states to come together and address international issues. Comprised of all 193 member states, the General Assembly meets annually in a regular session from September to December. During these sessions, member states discuss a variety of topics, including international peace and security, economic, social, cultural, and humanitarian cooperation, and the admission of new member states.

The General Assembly also elects the non-permanent members of the Security Council and the members of the International Court of Justice; it is chaired by a President, elected for a one-year term. The decisions made by this organ are a significant means for member states to express their collective will on international issues and play a central role in the work of the United Nations.

In addition to the regular sessions, the General Assembly may hold special sessions to address urgent issues as needed. All member states have equal representation in the General Assembly, and decisions on most issues are made by a two-thirds majority. The General Assembly also serves as a platform for member states to discuss and coordinate on issues related to the maintenance of international peace and security.

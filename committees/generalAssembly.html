Overview

The General Assembly (GA) is a principal organ of the United Nations (UN), consisting of all 193 member states. It is responsible for addressing and finding solutions to global issues such as peace and security, economic development, and human rights. The GA meets once a year in regular session and can also convene special sessions as needed.

The GA is divided into two main bodies: the Plenary, which consists of all member states and is responsible for conducting general debate and making decisions on issues, and the Main Committees, which consist of smaller groups of member states and deal with more specialized issues. There are also a number of other subsidiary bodies and committees that report to the GA and provide recommendations on various issues.

Decisions of the GA are made through a voting process, with each member state having one vote. The GA can adopt resolutions, which are non-binding recommendations, or decisions, which are binding on member states. The GA also elects members to various UN bodies and committees and appoints the UN Secretary-General. The GA plays a crucial role in the UN as it serves as a platform for member states to come together and address global issues. It allows for international cooperation and dialogue, and its decisions and resolutions can have a significant impact on international relations.
